\section{Purpose}
The objective of this study is to investigate the temporal characteristics of volcanic seismic activity recorded at the WIZ.NZ seismic station by applying ordinal-pattern-based feature extraction for time series clustering. The analysis uses continuous seismic observations collected between 2011 and 2022 from the GeoNet volcano monitoring network. The original seismic signals were sampled at \SI{100}{\hertz} and subsequently aggregated into 10-minute intervals. Each record contains the observation day and timestamp together with two seismic variables: the average ground displacement and the average Real-time Seismic Amplitude Measurement.

Ground displacement represents the average amplitude of ground motion measured by the seismometer during each 10-minute interval and is expressed in metres (m). In contrast, RSAM (Real-time Seismic Amplitude Measurement) provides a quantitative measure of the overall seismic signal amplitude during the same interval. Unlike displacement, RSAM is a relative measure of seismic activity rather than a physical quantity with SI units. It is widely used in volcano monitoring because it effectively summarizes changes in the level of seismic activity when individual earthquakes become difficult to distinguish due to continuous volcanic tremor or high earthquake rates. However, RSAM reflects the total seismic signal regardless of its origin and may also increase because of non-volcanic sources such as wind or other environmental noise~\cite{GeoNetRSAM}.

To investigate temporal variability, the seismic records are analysed at multiple temporal scales, including monthly datasets (January to December) and quarterly datasets. Each time series is transformed into an ordinal-pattern representation, from which several information-theoretic descriptors are computed, including Shannon entropy,  R\'enyi entropy, Tsallis entropy, statistical complexity, and Fisher information. These complementary measures quantify different aspects of the temporal structure and complexity of the seismic signals, enabling comparisons of temporal behaviour across different months, quarters, and years. The resulting feature vectors are subsequently used to assess the effectiveness of ordinal-pattern-based representations for clustering seismic time series exhibiting similar temporal characteristics.

The study considers two types of seismic measurements, displacement and Real-time Seismic Amplitude Measurement (RSAM), derived from seismometer recordings sampled at \SI{100}{\hertz} and aggregated into 10-minute intervals from the year 2011 to 2022. Features derived from Shannon entropy, R\'enyi entropy, Tsallis entropy, statistical complexity, and Fisher information provide a comprehensive description of the underlying dynamics and enable the comparison of temporal patterns across different time periods. By examining these feature representations, the study aims to identify similarities and differences in seismic activity and assess the usefulness of ordinal-pattern-based features for analyzing and clustering seismic time series.

\subsection{Data Representation}

The seismic dataset consists of temporal observations recorded at the WIZ seismic station in New Zealand. Two indicators of volcanic seismic activity were analysed: the Real-time Seismic Amplitude Measurement (RSAM) and the average ground displacement. The data span the period from 2011 to 2022 and were obtained from continuous seismic monitoring records. The original seismometer recordings were sampled at \SI{100}{\hertz} and aggregated into 10-minute intervals. Each observation is associated with a timestamp, enabling the construction of long-term time series that describe the temporal evolution of volcanic seismic activity. These time series provide the basis for the exploratory statistical analysis and the subsequent ordinal-pattern-based feature extraction presented in this study.

Prior to the ordinal pattern analysis, an exploratory statistical analysis was conducted to examine the temporal characteristics of the seismic time series. The analysis was performed separately for each calendar year (2011-2022), with the observations partitioned into monthly subsets to facilitate comparisons of temporal behaviour while reducing the influence of long-term inter-annual variability.

Tables~\ref{tab:annual_displacement_stats} and~\ref{tab:annual_rsam_stats} summarise the annual descriptive statistics of the displacement and RSAM time series for the period 2011-2022. 
For both variables, the distributions are positively skewed, as indicated by the mean consistently exceeding the median in most years, suggesting the presence of occasional high-amplitude seismic events. The displacement measurements exhibit substantial inter-annual variability, with the highest mean and standard deviation observed in 2022, while 2016 also shows elevated variability. Similarly, the RSAM measurements display considerable variability across years, with coefficients of variation ranging from approximately 0.65 to 3.45. The largest relative variability occurs in 2022, followed by 2021 and 2016. In contrast, 2011, 2012, 2014, 2015, 2017, and 2018 all exhibit relatively low coefficients of variation (less than 1), indicating comparatively more stable RSAM behaviour during those years. The minimum values for all variables are close to zero except in year 2015 and 2016, while the maximum values differ by several orders of magnitude, further highlighting the intermittent nature of high-amplitude seismic activity. Overall, these descriptive statistics demonstrate that both displacement and RSAM exhibit marked temporal variability over the study period, providing a basis for the subsequent analyses of temporal patterns using time series visualisation and ordinal-pattern-based information-theoretic measures.

\subsubsection{Information Theoretic Quantifiers}

For each ordinal pattern probability vector $\mathbf P=\{\mathbf p_\ell\}_{\ell=1}^{k}$, we evaluate a collection of 
global and local information measures.  
Given an estimator $\widehat{\mathbf P}=\{\widehat{\mathbf p}_\ell\}$ obtained from the empirical ordinal pattern frequencies, our goal is to study the behaviour of the entropy functionals $H(\widehat{ \mathbf P})$ and their associated complexity measures.

\paragraph{Permutation Entropy.}
Shannon entropy provides a quantitative measure of the uncertainty
encoded in the ordinal pattern distribution. Given the probability
vector
\(\widehat{\mathbf{p}} = (\widehat{p}_1,\widehat{p}_{2},\dots,\widehat{p}_k)\),
the normalized Shannon entropy, called Permutation entropy, is defined
as the Shannon entropy divided by its maximum value \(\log k\), i.e., \[
H(\widehat{\mathbf p})
=
-\frac{1}{\log k}
\sum_{\ell=1}^{k}
\widehat p_\ell \log \widehat p_\ell.
\] 
The normalization ensures that \(0\le H(\widehat{\mathbf p})\le1\), allowing comparisons across different embedding dimensions.

In the context of ordinal analysis, \(H(\widehat{\mathbf{p}})\) is commonly referred to as permutation entropy. It captures the degree of disorder in the symbolic dynamics: low values indicate strong regularity or determinism, while high values reflect randomness or
unpredictability. However, entropy alone cannot distinguish between randomness and structured complexity, motivating the introduction of complementary measures.

\paragraph{Tsallis entropy.}
For $q \in \mathbb{R}\setminus\{1\}$, the Tsallis entropy is given by
\[
H^{\mathrm{T}}_{q}(\widehat{\mathbf P})
= \frac{1}{q-1}\left(1 - \sum_{\ell=1}^{k} \widehat{\mathbf p}_\ell^{\,q}\right),
\]
which reduces to Shannon entropy in the limit $q \to 1$.  
A normalized version is obtained by dividing by its value under the uniform distribution.

\paragraph{Rényi entropy.}
For $q>0$, $q\neq 1$, the Rényi entropy is defined as
\[
H^{\mathrm{R}}_{q}(\widehat{P})
= \frac{1}{1-q}\log\!\left(\sum_{\ell=1}^{k} \widehat{\mathbf p}_\ell^{\,q}\right),
\]
and is likewise normalized using its maximum value at the uniform distribution.

\paragraph{Fisher Information Measure.}
To quantify local sensitivity in the ordinal pattern distribution and this is discussed by Felipe et.al.~\cite{Olivares2012b}, we adopt the discrete Fisher Information Measure proposed by Ferri et.al.~\cite{Ferri2009}:
\[
F(\widehat{\mathbf P})
= \frac{1}{2}\sum_{\ell=1}^{k-1}
\frac{(\widehat{\mathbf p}_{\ell+1}-\widehat{\mathbf p}_{\ell})^{2}}
{\widehat{\mathbf p}_{\ell+1}+\widehat{\mathbf p}_{\ell}}.
\]

This expression emphasizes differences between adjacent probabilities and therefore captures fine scale structure that entropy based measures may overlook.

\subsubsection{Disequilibrium}\label{disequilibrium}

All the entropies and FIM provide complementary information about the ordinal pattern distribution. However, neither descriptor quantifies the  degree to which the distribution deviates from the uniform distribution.
To incorporate this information, we consider the concept of disequilibrium introduced by~Lopez-Ruiz et al. (1995). In the ordinal pattern setting, the natural reference is the uniform distribution \(\mathbf{u}=(1/k,1/k,\ldots,1/k)\).

A widely adopted disequilibrium measure is based on the Jensen--Shannon divergence, which provides a symmetric, bounded, and information theoretically meaningful distance between \(\widehat{\mathbf{p}}\) and \(\mathbf{u}\): 
\[
J(\widehat{\mathbf{p}},\mathbf{u})
= H\!\left(\frac{\widehat{\mathbf{p}}+\mathbf{u}}{2}\right)
- \frac{1}{2}H(\widehat{\mathbf{p}})
- \frac{1}{2}\log(k).
\] 
The maximum possible divergence for a Jensen-Shannon divergence is 
\[
J_{\max}
= -2\left[\frac{(k+1)}{k}\log(k+1) - 2\log(2k) + \log k\right].
\] 
Using this, the normalized disequilibrium is defined as 
\[
Q(\widehat{\mathbf{p}}) = \frac{J(\widehat{\mathbf{p}},\mathbf{u})}{J_{\max}}.
\] 
This quantity satisfies that \(Q(\widehat{\mathbf{p}}) = 0\) for the uniform distribution (maximal disorder), and that \(Q(\widehat{\mathbf{p}}) = 1\) when all probability mass is concentrated on a single pattern (maximal deviation from equilibrium).

The same Jensen--Shannon-based disequilibrium is used throughout this study for constructing Shannon, Tsallis, Rényi, and Fisher complexity measures.

\subsubsection{Statistical Complexity Measures}
\label{complexity-measures}

Entropy and Fisher information quantify different aspects of the ordinal pattern distribution. Entropy measures describe the degree of uncertainty or disorder, whereas Fisher information captures local variations and sensitivity to changes in the distribution. However, these quantities
alone do not distinguish between complete randomness and structured organization. To account for both the amount of information and the departure from the uniform distribution, we consider statistical complexity measures.

Following the framework proposed Lamberti et al.~(2004)~\cite{lamberti2004intensive}, complexity is obtained as the product of an information measure and the normalized disequilibrium \(Q(\widehat{\mathbf p})\). This construction ensures that complexity is small both for highly ordered systems and for completely random systems, while attaining larger values for distributions exhibiting intermediate levels of organization.

The Shannon statistical complexity is defined as
\[
C_S(\widehat{\mathbf p})
=
H(\widehat{\mathbf p})
\,Q(\widehat{\mathbf p}),
\]
where \(H(\widehat{\mathbf p})\) is the normalized Shannon entropy.

Similarly, the Tsallis statistical complexity is
\[
C_T^{(q)}(\widehat{\mathbf p})
=
H_T^{(q)}(\widehat{\mathbf p})
\,Q(\widehat{\mathbf p}),
\]
where \(H_T^{(q)}(\widehat{\mathbf p})\) denotes the normalized Tsallis
entropy of order \(q\).

The Rényi statistical complexity is defined as
\[
C_R^{(q)}(\widehat{\mathbf p})
=
H_R^{(q)}(\widehat{\mathbf p})
\,Q(\widehat{\mathbf p}),
\]
where \(H_R^{(q)}(\widehat{\mathbf p})\) denotes the normalized Rényi
entropy of order \(q\).

Finally, a Fisher-based complexity measure is obtained by combining the Fisher Information Measure with the normalized disequilibrium:
\[
C_F(\widehat{\mathbf p})
=
F(\widehat{\mathbf p})
\,Q(\widehat{\mathbf p}).
\]

These complexity measures combine information about the uncertainty, local structure, and deviation from equilibrium of the ordinal pattern distribution, providing complementary descriptors for characterizing the underlying dynamics of the time series.


\subsection{Feature Extraction from Seismic Time Series}\label{feature_extraction}

Ordinal-pattern-based features are extracted from each time series according to the following procedure:

\begin{itemize}
	
	\item \textbf{Embedding:}
	A fixed embedding dimension ($D=5$) and time delay ($\tau=1$) are used to construct ordinal patterns from the seismic time series.
	
	\item \textbf{Temporal Aggregation:}
	The analysis considers monthly and quarterly subsets of the displacement and RSAM time series for the period 2011-2022.
	
	\item \textbf{Feature Extraction:}
	For each time series, the ordinal pattern distribution is obtained and the following quantities are computed:
	
	\begin{itemize}
		
		\item \emph{Entropy Measures:}
		Shannon entropy, R\'enyi entropy, Tsallis entropy, and Fisher information measure.
		
		\item \emph{Complexity Measures:}
		The corresponding Shannon, R\'enyi, Tsallis, and Fisher complexity measures.
		
		\item \emph{Disequilibrium:}
		The Jensen--Shannon disequilibrium associated with the ordinal pattern distribution.
		
		\item \emph{Variance Measures:}
		Variance estimates for selected entropy and complexity measures to assess feature stability.
		
		\item \emph{Confidence Intervals:}
		Confidence intervals for entropy and complexity measures are computed to quantify estimation uncertainty.
		
	\end{itemize}
	
\end{itemize}

The analysis is based on the following thirteen ordinal-pattern features:

\begin{itemize}
	\item $H_{\text{Shannon}}$,
	$H_{\text{Rényi}}$,
	$H_{\text{Tsallis}}$,
	$H_{\text{Fisher}}$,
	
	\item $C_{\text{Shannon}}$,
	$C_{\text{Rényi}}$,
	$C_{\text{Tsallis}}$,
	$C_{\text{Fisher}}$,
	
	\item Disequilibrium,
	
	\item $\mathrm{Var}(H_{\text{Shannon}})$,
	$\mathrm{Var}(H_{\text{Rényi}})$,
	$\mathrm{Var}(H_{\text{Tsallis}})$,
	
	\item $\mathrm{Var}(C_{\text{Shannon}})$.
\end{itemize}
